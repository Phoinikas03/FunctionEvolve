## Step 3: Compare Basis Functions

The GT has the form:
$$f(P) = aP - a e^{-\lambda P} P + \frac{a}{K_1} P^2 - \frac{a}{K_1 K_2} P^3$$

The critical term is $P \cdot e^{-\lambda P}$, which is an **exponential function** of $P$.

The candidate $g(P) = r P^{\alpha}(1 - (P/K)^{\theta}) + c$ is a sum of **power-law terms**: $r P^{\alpha} - r P^{\alpha+\theta}/K^{\theta} + c$.

No matter what values we assign to $r, K, \theta, \alpha, c$, the candidate is always a sum of at most two power-law terms plus a constant. It can never produce:
1. A term like $P \cdot e^{-\lambda P}$ (exponential in $P$)
2. Three independent polynomial terms ($P$, $P^2$, $P^3$) simultaneously

The exponential term $e^{-\lambda P}$ is **linearly independent** from any power-law function $P^{\alpha}$. By the principle of linear independence of basis functions, a sum of power-law terms cannot equal a function containing an exponential term.

Even if we tried to match just the polynomial part ($aP + \frac{a}{K_1}P^2 - \frac{a}{K_1 K_2}P^3$), the candidate only provides two power-law terms ($P^{\alpha}$ and $P^{\alpha+\theta}$), which cannot match three independent polynomial terms unless we can set $c$ to absorb one — but $c$ is a constant, not a function of $P$.

## Conclusion

The candidate cannot reproduce the $P \cdot e^{-\lambda P}$ term present in the GT. Power-law functions and exponential functions are fundamentally different basis functions and cannot be made equivalent by any choice of constants.
