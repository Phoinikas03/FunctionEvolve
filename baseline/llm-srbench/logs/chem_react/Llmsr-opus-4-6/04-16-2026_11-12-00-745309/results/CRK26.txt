### Can $k_2 \cdot (k_3 - A)^m + k_4$ equal $0.1365 \cdot \sin(\sqrt{A})$?

The expression $(k_3 - A)^m$ is a power function. For any real value of $m$, $(k_3 - A)^m$ is a power/algebraic function. The function $\sin(\sqrt{A})$ is a transcendental function involving a trigonometric composition with a square root.

By the principle of linear independence of basis functions, a power function $(k_3 - A)^m$ (which is algebraic for rational $m$, or a generalized power for irrational $m$) plus a constant $k_4$ **cannot** equal $\sin(\sqrt{A})$ for all values of $A$.

More specifically:
- $\sin(\sqrt{A})$ oscillates (though for $A \geq 0$, it oscillates as $A$ grows)
- $(k_3 - A)^m + k_4$ is monotonic or has at most one turning point for any fixed $m$, and does not oscillate

Even if we consider that in a restricted domain $A$ might be small, the Taylor expansion of $\sin(\sqrt{A}) = \sqrt{A} - \frac{A^{3/2}}{6} + \ldots$ involves half-integer powers, while $(k_3 - A)^m$ expanded around $A = 0$ gives $k_3^m - m k_3^{m-1} A + \ldots$, which for any single value of $m$ cannot reproduce the $\sqrt{A}$ leading term (unless $m = 1/2$ and $k_3 = 0$, giving $(-A)^{1/2}$ which is imaginary for $A > 0$).

There is no way to make a power function equal a sine-of-square-root function across the domain. The candidate is **missing the $\sin(\sqrt{A})$ basis function**, and the available terms cannot substitute for it.

Setting $k_2 = 0$ would eliminate the reverse reaction term, but then $k_4$ alone (a constant) cannot match $0.1365 \cdot \sin(\sqrt{A})$ which varies with $A$.
