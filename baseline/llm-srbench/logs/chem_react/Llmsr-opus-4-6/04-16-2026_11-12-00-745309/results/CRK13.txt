## Equivalence Check

For the candidate to match the GT, we need:
$$p_0 \cdot A^{p_1} \cdot e^{p_2 A} + p_3 \cdot A + p_4 = -0.23237 \cdot A + 0.39588 \cdot \sin(0.52657 \cdot A)$$

The linear terms can be matched: set $p_3 = -0.23237$.
The constant term: set $p_4 = 0$ (since GT has no constant term, and sin(0) = 0).

This leaves us needing:
$$p_0 \cdot A^{p_1} \cdot e^{p_2 A} = 0.39588 \cdot \sin(0.52657 \cdot A)$$

Now, the left side is a product of a power function and an exponential function. The right side is a sinusoidal function.

**Key question:** Can $A^{p_1} \cdot e^{p_2 A}$ ever equal $\sin(c \cdot A)$ for all $A \geq 0$?

No. The function $A^{p_1} \cdot e^{p_2 A}$ is a monotonic function (for fixed real parameters) multiplied by a sign, or at most has a single extremum. It cannot oscillate. The sine function $\sin(0.52657 \cdot A)$ is periodic and oscillates, crossing zero at $A = k\pi/0.52657$ for integer $k$.

For example:
- $\sin(0.52657 \cdot A) = 0$ at $A = 0, \approx 5.966, \approx 11.932, ...$
- Between these zeros, sin changes sign.

Meanwhile, $A^{p_1} \cdot e^{p_2 A}$ (for $A > 0$) is either always positive or always negative (depending on sign of $p_0$), and cannot have multiple zeros for $A > 0$ (it can only be zero at $A = 0$ if $p_1 > 0$).

By the **linear independence** principle (Rule 3), exponential/power-law functions and trigonometric functions are linearly independent basis functions. A product of power and exponential functions cannot reproduce a sinusoidal function.

## Conclusion

The candidate's basis functions (power-law × exponential, linear, constant) cannot reproduce the sinusoidal component $\sin(c \cdot A)$ present in the GT. These are fundamentally different function classes, and no choice of parameters can make them equivalent for all values of A.
