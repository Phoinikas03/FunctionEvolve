## Comparison

The candidate has $k_3 \cdot A \cdot e^{-|k_4| t}$, which matches the GT's $-0.1207 \cdot A \cdot e^{-0.0686t}$ by setting $k_3 = -0.1207$ and $k_4 = 0.0686$. ✓

### Term 1 (GT: $\sqrt{A}$):
The candidate's saturating term is $-k_1 \frac{A^{n_1}}{K_m + A^{n_1}}$. This is a rational function of $A^{n_1}$. No matter what values we assign to $k_1$, $n_1$, and $K_m$, this is always a rational function — it can never equal $\sqrt{A}$ (which is an algebraic but non-rational function that grows unboundedly). The Michaelis-Menten term is bounded (approaches $k_1$ as $A \to \infty$), while $\sqrt{A} \to \infty$. So this term **cannot** reproduce $\sqrt{A}$.

Could we try to use the reverse term to produce $\sqrt{A}$? If we set $n_2 = 0.5$ and $A_{eq} = 0$, we'd get $k_2 \cdot \text{sign}(-A) \cdot |A|^{0.5} = -k_2 \sqrt{A}$ (for $A > 0$). This could match $-0.1207\sqrt{A}$ with $k_2 = 0.1207$. ✓

But then we've used the reverse term for $\sqrt{A}$, and we have no term left to produce $\sin(\log(A+1))$.

### Term 2 (GT: $\sin(\log(A+1))$):
The GT requires $\sin(\log(A+1))$, which is a transcendental oscillatory function. The candidate has:
- A rational/Michaelis-Menten term (bounded, monotonic — cannot oscillate)
- A power-law term (monotonic — cannot oscillate)
- An exponential decay term (already matched)

Neither the saturating term nor the power-law term can produce $\sin(\log(A+1))$. The sine function oscillates (albeit slowly in log-space), and no combination of rational functions and power laws can reproduce this behavior exactly.

Even if we set the saturating term's coefficient $k_1 = 0$ (eliminating it), we still only have a power-law term and the exponential term — neither can produce $\sin(\log(A+1))$.

The candidate lacks the necessary basis function $\sin(\log(A+1))$. Since trigonometric/oscillatory functions are linearly independent from rational functions and power laws, there is no assignment of constants that makes the candidate equivalent to the GT.
